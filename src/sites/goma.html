GOMA GLASGOW
Interactive Heritage Visualisation

3D modelling & scanning // RT3D // Unity physically based shading

The aim of this project was to create an interactive 3D visualisation of the front part of Glasgow’s Gallery of Modern Art (GoMA) by using a combination of conventional 3D modelling and photogrammetry-based reproduction techniques.
The reconstructed parts comprise the facade of the Gallery’s 1780 Royal exchange building and forefront equestrian statue of the Duke of Wellington. The final models were staged in Unity together with low-poly models of the surrounding Glaswegian cityscape (Google Maps models).
The model can be explored interactively with a first-person controller.
From High-Res 3D Scan to Game Art Asset
While the facade's and statue's general geometry was reproduced using conventional 3D modelling techniques, the reconstruction of more filigree parts, e.g. the bronze plates on the statue's socket, was realised using a photogrammetry-based 3D scanning workflow.
Photogrammety techniques allow to reproduce real-world objects from a series of photographs to create 3D models of stunning detail. However, the resulting model meshes are often too large to be used in real-time 3D (RT3D) visualisations: a high-res photogrammetry mesh can easily exceed 1m vertices, but RT3D engines such as Unity only support meshes of up to 64K vertices. Hence, to be usable as a 3D asset in Unity, a photogrammetry mesh first needs to be downsampled to 6% of its original resolution. However, a brute-force mesh reduction at this scale would also result in the loss of the model's valuable details.
Therefore, to be able to use photogrammetric models in this project, a workflow was developed for reducing high-res meshes to the required size while at the same time preserving as much of their rich artistic detail as possible.
The steps of the final workflow are demonstrated below:
  • A photogrammetric analysis of 38 photos produces a high-res point cloud of ca. 10m points (Agisoft Photoscan).
  • The resulting high-res mesh counts about 1.1m vertices.
  • The texture is rendered at 4k resolution.
  • A close-up view of high-res mesh and texture result (12x zoom) reveals the model's high level of detail.
  • The original point cloud is used to reconstruct a low-res version of the mesh of ca. 20k vertices via
    Poisson Surface Reconstruction (Meshlab).
  • The details of the high-res mesh are transferred to the low-res version as a normal map (using Mudbox).
    The map is then filtered for noise and artifacts.
  • The texture UVs of the high-res mesh are transferred to the low-res version. As a finishing step, the texture undergoes some post-processing, i.e. stitching artifacts are retouched and blank spots are filled in.
  • The finished low-res bronze plate (20K vertices, 4K texture map, 2K normal map)
The workflow was used to create the statue's four bronze plates, the GoMA's entrance doors and the Corinthian pillar capitals:

Project Specs
PLATFORM: Standalone application (Win / Mac)
KEY METHODS: Photogrammetry, mesh resolution reduction with detail transfer, physically based shading
SOFTWARE USED: Unity3D, Agisoft Photoscan, Autodesk 3ds Max, Autodesk Mudbox, Meshlab, Quixel Suite, Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop
